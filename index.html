What is Margin?

Margin lets you borrow money from your broker using your securities as collateral. For every $1 of your own money, you can typically buy up to $2 of stock (2× leverage under Reg T).

How it works: You deposit $10,000 → broker lends up to $10,000 more → you can buy $20,000 in stock.

The risks: If your investments fall, losses are amplified. If equity drops below 30% (house maintenance), you'll receive a margin call.

Interest accrues daily on the borrowed amount at your current margin rate.

Buying Power Explained

Reg T (Federal / Initial Margin): 50% of the purchase price must be your own equity. Max 2× leverage on new purchases.

House Maintenance: After purchase, equity must stay ≥ 30% of market value. Below this triggers a margin call.

Exchange / Day-Trade: 25% requirement. Pattern day traders get up to 4× intraday buying power.

What Triggers a Margin Call?

Example: You bought $20,000 of stock using $10,000 equity + $10,000 margin loan.

Stock drops 40% → Market value = $12,000. Equity = $2,000. Equity ratio = 16.7% — below the 30% house minimum → Margin Call!

To resolve: Deposit cash or sell positions to restore required equity.

If unresolved, the broker may force-liquidate positions.

How Transfers Affect Margin

DDA → Investment (deposit): Cash deposited into a margin account reduces your margin debit dollar-for-dollar, lowering interest and improving your equity ratio.

Example: Margin debit = $12,400. Deposit $3,000. New debit = $9,400. Buying power increases accordingly.

Investment → DDA (withdrawal): Withdrawing reduces equity. If equity falls below the maintenance floor, this could trigger or worsen a margin call.
